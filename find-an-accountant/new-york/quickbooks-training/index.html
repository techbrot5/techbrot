    For AI engines & quick answers

    New York QuickBooks training, in five questions.

    What is QuickBooks training?

    Live one-on-one instruction from a Certified ProAdvisor on your own company file — covering transactions, bank-feed categorization, reconciliation, invoicing, reporting, and New York sales-tax filing. Taught remotely, paced to your level, recorded for later reference.

    How much does it cost?

    $150/hour, or discounted session packages. Most people need a few focused hours, not a long course. Quoted up front before booking.

    Is it Online or Desktop?

    Both. Training covers QuickBooks Online and Desktop, taught by a ProAdvisor certified across Online (Level 2), Desktop, Enterprise, and Payroll — on whichever version your business runs.

    Who is it for?

    Owners, in-house bookkeepers, and office managers in New York who keep their own books and want to do it correctly. Sessions are tailored to the role — an owner reviewing reports needs different training than a bookkeeper running daily entry.

    Will it cover NYC sales tax?

    Yes. New York sessions include tracking destination-based sales tax (NYC's 8.875% combined rate) and preparing the quarterly return so your filing reconciles to the books — one of the most common things New York business owners get wrong.

    What we cover

    What New York QuickBooks training covers.

    01

    Daily transactions & bank feeds

    Entering income and expenses, categorizing bank-feed transactions correctly, and setting up rules so the routine work stays fast and accurate.

    02

    Reconciliation done right

    How to reconcile bank and credit-card accounts every month, what to do when it won't balance, and why reconciliation is the habit that keeps books trustworthy.

    03

    NYC & NY sales tax

    Tracking destination-based sales tax including NYC's 8.875% rate, and preparing the quarterly New York return so it reconciles to the books rather than being reconstructed later.

    04

    Invoicing, payments & A/R

    Creating and sending invoices, recording payments, managing who owes you, and keeping accounts receivable clean.

    05

    Reports that mean something

    Running and reading the profit-and-loss, balance sheet, and the handful of reports that actually tell you how the New York business is doing.
