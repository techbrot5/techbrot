    Quick answers

    New York consulting accounting, in five questions.

    What's different about accounting for a consulting firm?

    Profit lives in utilization and project margin, not the bank balance. Consulting accounting tracks billable rates, recognizes retainer and milestone revenue in the right period, and separates pass-through costs from marked-up ones — so you see which clients and projects actually make money.

    Do New York consultants charge sales tax?

    It depends on the service. Pure management consulting is generally not taxable in New York, but services like certain information services, software, or staffing can be. We set QuickBooks up to tax what's taxable and leave the rest — so your return reconciles.

    What about clients in other states?

    That's where nexus appears. A NY consultancy serving out-of-state clients — or with remote staff in other states — can trigger income-tax and sales-tax nexus elsewhere. We track the data your CPA needs to manage multi-state exposure.

    What does it cost?

    $400–$2,500+/mo, fixed-fee against a written scope, set by transaction volume, number of accounts, and project-reporting complexity.

    Can you report profit by client and project?

    Yes — that's the core of it. Using QuickBooks classes, projects, and tags, a named Certified ProAdvisor structures the file so you can pull profitability by client, project, or service line, not just a company-wide P&L.

    Why NY consulting books mislead

    Three places New York consultancies lose the real numbers.

    A busy, profitable-looking firm can still be subsidizing its worst clients. Knowing which of these you're in tells us where to start.

    • Client profit is invisible

      No project or client costing.

      Most common · almost every firm

      The problem: Revenue and labor land in company-wide totals, not against clients or projects. You know the firm was profitable this year — you don't know which engagements earned it and which ran over budget on your time. You price the next proposal blind.

      The fix: Profit tracked by client and project using QuickBooks classes, projects, and tags — billable time and direct costs mapped to each engagement.

      Honest read: If you can't pull margin on a single completed engagement in under a minute, this is your starting point.

    • Revenue is booked wrong

      Retainers and milestones recognized as cash-in.

      High impact · retainer & project work

      The problem: A six-month retainer paid up front isn't six months of profit today — it's deferred revenue earned over the term. Book it as cash-in and the P&L swings wildly, margins look unreal, and a mid-year exit or audit gets messy.

      The fix: Retainer and milestone revenue recognized across the period it's earned, with deferred revenue tracked on the balance sheet.

      Honest read: If a big invoice makes one month look spectacular and the next look broke, your revenue timing is off.

    • NY & multi-state risk

      No handle on nexus or taxable services.

      Rising risk · remote & multi-state firms

      The problem: The moment a NY consultancy serves out-of-state clients or hires remote staff elsewhere, it can create income- and sales-tax nexus in those states. And not all consulting is sales-tax-free in New York — some information and staffing services are taxable. Both get missed until a notice arrives.

      The fix: QuickBooks set to tax the services that are taxable in NY, with the by-state revenue and payroll data your CPA needs to manage multi-state exposure.

      Honest read: If you bill clients in three states and have never looked at nexus, the exposure is already accruing.
