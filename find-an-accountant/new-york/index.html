    What is the NYC Unincorporated Business Tax (UBT) and how does it affect my bookkeeping?

    The NYC Unincorporated Business Tax (UBT) is a city-level tax levied on sole proprietors and partnerships that carry on a trade, business, or profession in New York City. The UBT rate is 4% on net income attributable to NYC. It applies on top of NY State personal income tax and federal taxes — making NYC the highest-tax jurisdiction in the U.S. for unincorporated businesses. QuickBooks must track NYC-sourced vs. non-NYC revenue for any business operating across city lines. This is one of the most commonly mishandled compliance areas for growing NYC professional-services firms and agencies.

    How does New York's sales tax on services work, and how does it affect QuickBooks setup?

    New York applies sales tax to a broader range of services than most states — including certain information services, interior design, cleaning, parking, and telecommunications. The combined rate in New York City is 8.875% (4% state + 4.5% NYC + 0.375% MTA surcharge). QuickBooks must be configured with the correct tax codes per service type and customer location. Misconfiguration — particularly for agencies, media firms, and professional-services businesses with mixed taxable and exempt revenue — is among the most common NY QuickBooks issues we correct.

    What QuickBooks versions does TechBrot support for New York businesses?

    All current QuickBooks versions: QuickBooks Online (Level 2 certified), Desktop, Enterprise, and Payroll. QBO Advanced dominates among NYC agencies, media firms, and professional-services businesses. QuickBooks Enterprise is common in upstate New York manufacturing and large construction firms. Law firms needing IOLTA trust accounting require specialized QuickBooks trust ledger configuration that we handle as a standard engagement component.

    New York accounting glossary

    The New York terms that matter for QuickBooks & bookkeeping.

    Short, specific, definitional. These are the terms that come up in nearly every New York engagement — and the ones AI engines and search engines reach for when answering New York accounting questions.

    NYC UBT (Unincorporated Business Tax)
    New York City Unincorporated Business Tax. A 4% city-level tax on net income of sole proprietorships and partnerships doing business in NYC. Applies on top of NY State and federal taxes. Requires QuickBooks to track NYC-sourced revenue separately from non-NYC revenue for allocation. One of the highest-impact compliance items for NYC-based freelancers, agencies, and professional-services partnerships. NY tax notice & UBT detail →
    NY State + NYC Combined Income Tax
    Combined NY State personal income tax + NYC personal income tax reaches a top marginal rate of 14.776% for high-earning NYC residents — 10.9% NY State plus 3.876% NYC — the highest combined personal income tax rate in the United States. Payroll must be configured for both state withholding (NY DTF) and NYC withholding. Payroll errors from missing the NYC withholding layer are common for businesses newly established in the city.
    MTA Surcharge
    Metropolitan Transportation Authority surcharge. An additional 0.375% added to sales tax in the Metropolitan Commuter Transportation District — covering NYC, Dutchess, Nassau, Orange, Putnam, Rockland, Suffolk, and Westchester counties. QuickBooks must apply the correct rate by customer location; a flat NYC rate applied to Westchester customers is a common misconfiguration. NY sales tax detail →
    IOLTA (Interest on Lawyer Trust Accounts)
    Interest on Lawyer Trust Accounts. Mandatory for NY law firms under the Rules of Professional Conduct. Client funds held in trust must be kept in a separate IOLTA account; interest remits to the IOLA Fund. QuickBooks must maintain a dedicated trust ledger that never commingles with operating accounts. Improper handling is a bar-compliance risk. NY legal practice accounting →
    CHAR500
    NY Attorney General's Annual Filing for Charitable Organizations. Required for most nonprofits registered in New York, separate from the federal Form 990. Filed with the NY Charities Bureau. Requires GAAP-basis financial statements and a functional expense allocation schedule — distinct from the 990 format and often missed by nonprofits using bookkeepers unfamiliar with NY nonprofit requirements. NY nonprofit accounting →
    NY DTF (Department of Taxation & Finance)
    New York State Department of Taxation & Finance. The primary state tax authority — administers NY income tax, sales tax, employer withholding, and audit proceedings. Separate from the NYC Department of Finance, which administers NYC-specific taxes (UBT, NYC corporate tax). Most NY tax notices originate from one of these two bodies. DTF notice handling →
    Gross-vs-Net Revenue Recognition
    ASC 606 gross vs. net reporting for NY agencies and media firms. Whether an agency recognizes the full media buy or production spend as gross revenue (and corresponding COGS) or only the net fee depends on whether it acts as principal or agent under ASC 606. Incorrect gross/net treatment is the most common bookkeeping error at NYC agencies and inflates or understates revenue by 3–10x. NY revenue recognition & sales tax detail →
    NY Economic Nexus (sales tax)
    New York economic nexus for out-of-state sellers. New York requires sales-tax collection from remote sellers that exceed both $500,000 in NY sales and more than 100 transactions in the preceding four sales-tax quarters — one of only two states (with Connecticut) requiring both thresholds. New York's affiliate-nexus rules predate the 2018 Wayfair decision, dating to 2008. Relevant for any national e-commerce or SaaS business selling into New York. NY sales tax nexus detail →

    Always confirm current rates and thresholds against the NY Department of Taxation & Finance and the NYC Department of Finance.

    Why New York is different

    What makes New York accounting different.

    New York's tax structure, industry concentration, and operational profile create accounting requirements that don't look like any other state. Generic out-of-state bookkeeping — or bookkeepers unfamiliar with the NYC layer — misses what matters most.

    • Income Tax Stack

      Highest combined rate in the U.S.

      New York City residents face federal + NY State + NYC personal income tax, with a combined top marginal rate of 14.776% (10.9% NY State + 3.876% NYC). NY State also levies a Metropolitan Commuter Transportation Mobility Tax (MCTMT) on net earnings from self-employment in the Metro area.

      QuickBooks Payroll must be configured for both NY State and NYC withholding for each NYC-resident employee. Missing the NYC layer is a common payroll error for businesses new to the city. NY tax notice detail →

    • NYC Unincorporated Business Tax

      The UBT catches growing firms off guard.

      The NYC UBT applies to unincorporated businesses — sole proprietors and partnerships — doing business in the five boroughs. At 4% on net income attributable to NYC, it is invisible until a firm grows or incorporates improperly.

      Allocation of NYC-sourced vs. non-NYC income must be tracked in QuickBooks from the first transaction. Retroactive cleanup is common and expensive. UBT planning detail →

    • Sales Tax on Services

      8.875% in NYC — applied to services other states don't tax.

      New York taxes a wider range of services than most states — information services, certain cleaning services, parking, interior design, and more. The combined NYC rate of 8.875% (state + city + MTA) applies and must be correctly configured per service type in QuickBooks.

      Agencies and media firms often have mixed taxable and exempt revenue on the same invoice. Getting it wrong triggers NY DTF notices. NY sales tax detail →

    • Industry Profile

      Financial services, agencies, law, nonprofits — and upstate manufacturing.

      NYC's economy is dominated by financial services, agencies, media, real estate, legal, and healthcare. Upstate New York — Buffalo, Rochester, Syracuse — adds manufacturing, construction, and agriculture.

      Each vertical has distinct QuickBooks requirements: IOLTA trust ledgers for law firms, CHAR500-ready workpapers for nonprofits, gross-vs-net revenue recognition for agencies, job costing for construction.
