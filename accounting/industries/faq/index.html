    Cluster 01

    Why industry matters.

    What industry-specific accounting actually means, where it shows up in the books, and when specialization is essential vs when a competent generalist will do.

    1. Q.01 Why does industry-specific accounting matter?

      Industries have distinctive accounting patterns that generic bookkeeping completely misses. Revenue recognition varies wildly: SaaS deferred revenue under ASC 606, construction percentage-of-completion, agencies gross vs net (principal vs agent), fitness memberships as deferred revenue, nonprofits restricted vs unrestricted under ASC 958. Operational economics differ: restaurants need prime cost, manufacturers need BOM costing, home services need revenue per truck per day, law firms need IOLTA trust accounting.

      Generic accountants handle the universal layer (bookkeeping, financial statements) but miss the industry layer (industry-specific KPIs, industry-specific CoA, industry-specific GAAP requirements). The result: books that pass a generic audit but don’t produce the operational insights the business actually needs. Industry specialization separates competent operators from generalists. See /accounting/industries/ for the full coverage map.

    2. Q.02 Where does industry specialization actually show up in the books?

      Industry specialization shows up in five concrete places. (1) Chart of accounts design — industry-specific CoA structure that surfaces the right operational categories (a restaurant CoA breaks revenue by daypart and category; a SaaS CoA separates new ARR, expansion ARR, and renewals; a law firm CoA separates trust from operating). (2) Integration setup — connecting QuickBooks to the operational source-of-truth platforms (ServiceTitan for HVAC, MindBody for fitness, Toast or Square for restaurants, Shopify and Amazon for ecommerce). (3) Revenue recognition methodology — ASC 606 implementation tailored to the industry’s revenue patterns.

      (4) KPI dashboards — industry-specific metrics (RPTD, utilization, prime cost, MRR) calculated and reported monthly. (5) FAQ depth in proposals and advisory — the engagement structure anticipates the industry’s specific challenges rather than treating it generically. Generic bookkeepers handle steps 1 and 2 superficially and skip 3, 4, and 5 entirely. The difference is visible in monthly reporting: industry-specialist books surface what’s actually happening in the business; generic books surface accounting categories.

    3. Q.03 When is industry specialization essential vs when is generic accounting fine?

      Industry specialization is essential when the business has any of: distinctive revenue recognition (SaaS, construction, fitness, nonprofit, franchise), distinctive operational economics (restaurant prime cost, agency utilization, home services RPTD, trucking IFTA), distinctive GAAP requirements (nonprofit ASC 958, construction percentage-of-completion, leases under ASC 842), distinctive regulatory requirements (legal IOLTA, healthcare HIPAA-aware bookkeeping, multi-state franchise FDD reporting), or distinctive operational platforms (FSM, POS, PMS, AMS systems that drive the financial data).

      Industry specialization is less critical when the business is small ($300K–$1M revenue) with simple service operations, single-state, no inventory, no deferred revenue, generic SMB QuickBooks setup, and minimal complexity. At that scale, a competent generalist bookkeeper handles the work well. As businesses grow into complexity, the industry layer compounds in importance. Most businesses that engage us recognize the industry layer is missing before they engage; some realize it only after switching from a generalist and seeing the difference in monthly reporting.

    4. Q.04 What’s the difference between industry-specialist and industry-aware accounting?

      Industry-aware means the accountant has worked with businesses in your industry before and avoids the obvious mistakes — they know what a chart of accounts should roughly look like, what the major operational platforms are, what GAAP touchpoints exist. Industry-specialist means the firm has worked with dozens or hundreds of businesses in the industry, has built reusable engagement models for that industry, maintains a current view of industry-specific platforms and integrations, and treats the industry as a continued specialization rather than incidental coverage.

      The practical difference: industry-aware accountants handle the major touchpoints correctly but may miss sub-vertical nuances or recent platform changes. Industry specialists handle nuances, sub-verticals, and platform evolution because they see the industry continuously. For most growing businesses, industry-aware is enough early; industry-specialist matters more as the business scales into complexity. TechBrot positions as industry-specialist across 17 industries — we maintain dedicated pages, FAQ depth, and engagement models for each.

    5. Q.05 Does industry specialization cost more than generic bookkeeping?

      Industry-specialist engagements typically price similarly to industry-aware engagements once you compare like-for-like scope. The fee differential isn’t the industry overlay — it’s the depth of the engagement (KPI dashboards, integration management, revenue recognition discipline, advisory layer). A generic bookkeeper at $300/month delivers transaction categorization and basic reconciliation; an industry-specialist firm at $800–$1,500/month delivers all of that plus the industry-specific reporting that actually drives decisions.

      The economic comparison is not generic vs specialist at the same price point — it’s the value differential of correct industry treatment. Construction books without WIP tracking pass a tax filing but provide no operational visibility; with proper WIP they reveal project margin in real time. SaaS books without ASC 606 discipline create misleading early-month profitability; with proper deferred revenue they show actual unit economics. The marginal cost of industry specialization typically returns 5–20x in better decisions and avoided mistakes. See /pricing/ for canonical pricing.

    Cluster 02

    Choosing the right industry fit.

    Multi-industry businesses, sub-vertical positioning, holding-company structures, and when industry classification matters less than it seems.

    1. Q.06 What if my business doesn’t fit cleanly into one industry?

      Multi-industry businesses are common — agencies with productized SaaS arms, retailers with ecommerce channels, restaurants with catering plus retail products, construction firms with property management. We handle these through Class or Department tracking in QuickBooks, with a chart-of-accounts structure that lets each business line report separately while still rolling up to a consolidated view.

      Pricing reflects complexity but generally scales sub-linearly — a business spanning two industries doesn’t pay double a single-industry engagement; it pays a complexity premium roughly 30–50% above the dominant-industry baseline. The discovery call identifies the dominant model (typically the one driving 60%+ of revenue or complexity), maps the secondary lines, and scopes the engagement to handle both without forcing the business into a category that doesn’t fit. Multi-industry handling is one of the places industry specialization actually helps — you need a firm fluent enough in multiple industries to see what each requires.

    2. Q.07 My industry isn’t on your list — does that mean you can’t help?

      Most industries fit close enough to our 17 published verticals that engagement structure works. Common “not on the list” cases that actually fit: insurance agencies (similar to professional services with some agency mechanics), consulting practices (covered by professional services), creative agencies and design studios (covered by agency), specialty trade contractors (covered by home services or construction depending on scope), tourism and hospitality (covered by restaurant for operational similarity), independent medical specialties (covered by healthcare), wholesale and distribution (covered by manufacturing or retail with inventory overlay).

      The discovery call maps your business to the nearest fit and identifies any gaps in the standard engagement model. If the fit is genuinely poor (see Q.22 for what we don’t serve well), we’ll route to a specialist firm with the right depth. Industry coverage isn’t about checking boxes — it’s about whether we can actually serve your business well.

    3. Q.08 Do you serve multi-entity businesses or holding companies?

      Yes — multi-entity and multi-industry engagements are common, particularly for holding companies, multi-location operators, and businesses with related-party operations across different verticals. Multi-entity engagement scope includes: separate QuickBooks files per entity (or multi-company structure where appropriate), consolidated financial reporting across entities, intercompany transaction reconciliation, equity tracking across entities, and entity-level KPIs reported separately plus consolidated reporting.

      Common multi-industry patterns we handle: franchise operators with 5–20+ units across multiple brands; real estate holdings combining property management and operating companies; family offices with operating businesses plus investment entities; service businesses with both retail and B2B divisions. Pricing scales with complexity but more efficiently than single-entity multipliers — some work (CoA design, integration setup, reporting standards) applies across all entities. For coordinated fractional CFO work across multi-entity groups, see the advisory hub.

    4. Q.09 How do you handle businesses with sub-verticals?

      Many industries have meaningful sub-verticals that change the engagement model. Ecommerce sub-verticals: Amazon FBA sellers (FBA fee structure, inventory in fulfillment centers, multi-state nexus), Shopify direct-to-consumer (payment processor reconciliation, refund handling), wholesale ecommerce (B2B terms, Net 30 receivables), subscription ecommerce (deferred revenue under ASC 606). SaaS sub-verticals: pure SaaS subscription, usage-based pricing, hybrid SaaS+services, marketplace SaaS, vertical SaaS. Construction sub-verticals: residential remodeling, commercial general contracting, specialty trades, government contractors with certified payroll.

      Each industry page on our site covers the major sub-verticals with specific guidance. The engagement model adapts to your sub-vertical: a certified-payroll commercial GC has very different needs than a residential remodel shop, even though both are “construction.” The discovery call identifies the sub-vertical and scopes accordingly. See the individual industry pages for sub-vertical depth.

    5. Q.10 What if I’m in a regulated industry (legal, healthcare, financial services)?

      Regulated industries add a compliance overlay on top of standard accounting. For law firms, IOLTA trust accounting is the major regulatory dimension — trust funds must be tracked separately from operating funds, three-way reconciliation between trust ledger, bank statement, and client matter ledger is required, and state bar audit standards apply. We handle IOLTA. For healthcare practices, HIPAA-aware bookkeeping means PHI doesn’t flow through accounting systems; we maintain that discipline. For nonprofits, ASC 958 fund accounting (restricted vs unrestricted) is required and we maintain it.

      Regulated areas outside our coverage: registered investment advisor (RIA) compliance reporting to the SEC, broker-dealer regulatory accounting, mortgage originator compliance, cannabis state-by-state banking and regulatory accounting (see Q.22), and insurance carrier statutory accounting. For these, we recommend specialists with the specific regulatory depth required. Honest scope is the moat — the wrong firm in a regulated industry creates real risk.

    6. Q.11 What if my business is too small to need industry specialization?

      Genuine answer: businesses under roughly $300K revenue with simple operations may not need industry specialization yet. A solo consultant on cash basis, a small ecommerce business with only one channel, a single-location service business with no employees — these can run on competent generalist bookkeeping. The industry overlay produces value when complexity emerges, not before.

      That said, two things to consider: (1) generic books built at $300K revenue often need restructuring when the business hits $1M–$2M — the CoA wasn’t designed for the operations the business eventually has. Starting with industry-aware structure (even if engagement is minimal) prevents rebuild later. (2) If the business is in a high-complexity industry (construction, law, franchise, SaaS), the industry layer matters even at small size because the GAAP and regulatory requirements don’t scale down. The discovery call honestly assesses whether industry specialization is worth the cost at your current stage — sometimes the answer is “not yet,” and we’ll say so.

    Cluster 03

    Cross-industry patterns.

    The accounting patterns that recur across multiple industries — deferred revenue, project accounting, inventory, multi-state operations, integration discipline, and what they actually require.

    1. Q.12 Which industries require deferred revenue handling under ASC 606?

      ASC 606 deferred revenue applies anywhere a business collects payment before delivering service. The high-deferred-revenue industries on our coverage map: SaaS (annual or quarterly subscriptions paid upfront, recognized over the service period), fitness studios (annual memberships, class packages, personal training packs all collected upfront and earned over service), franchise operations (initial franchise fees recognized over the franchise term, not at signing under FASB ASC 606 changes), retail (gift cards as deferred revenue until redemption or breakage recognition), home services with prepaid maintenance agreements, professional services with retainer arrangements, and nonprofits with grants received before grant period.

      Without ASC 606 discipline, deferred revenue businesses look artificially profitable in early months and unprofitable when the deferred revenue runs off. With proper recognition, monthly P&L shows actual unit economics. Industry-specialist engagement includes ASC 606 implementation as default for industries that need it.

    2. Q.13 Which industries need project-based or job-cost accounting?

      Project accounting applies wherever revenue and cost are best understood at the project or job level rather than the aggregate. The industries that need it: construction (every project has its own P&L; WIP and percentage-of-completion required by GAAP), manufacturing (BOM costs, labor allocation, overhead absorption per work order), marketing agencies (client and project-level profitability), law firms (matter-level profitability), professional services (engagement-level profitability with utilization tracking), and most home services work that involves larger installations or commercial jobs.

      Project accounting requires CoA structure that separates direct costs (allocated to jobs) from overhead (allocated by a defined rate), QuickBooks Customer:Job structure or Class tracking, time tracking integration to allocate labor costs, and reporting that surfaces gross margin by job. Without project accounting, businesses don’t know which projects make money and which lose money. See /accounting/job-costing/ for the implementation methodology.

    3. Q.14 Which industries have the most complex inventory accounting?

      Inventory complexity sorts roughly into tiers. Simple inventory: small retail with limited SKUs, single location, periodic counts. Moderate inventory: multi-channel ecommerce (Shopify + Amazon + retail) with shared inventory pool, multi-location retail with inventory transfers, restaurants with food cost tracking. Complex inventory: manufacturing with raw materials, WIP, and finished goods (three-stage inventory), serial-number or lot-tracked items, FIFO/weighted-average/specific identification costing decisions, landed cost capitalization for imports.

      For most growing businesses, inventory eventually outgrows QuickBooks Online’s native inventory and requires either QuickBooks Enterprise (with Advanced Inventory) or a third-party inventory platform integrated with QuickBooks. The transition point is usually somewhere between $1M–$5M revenue depending on SKU count, multi-location complexity, and reporting requirements. We handle the platform selection and configuration as part of monthly bookkeeping engagements for inventory-heavy industries.

    4. Q.15 Which industries face the most multi-state complexity?

      Multi-state complexity shows up in three dimensions: sales tax nexus (where the business must collect sales tax), payroll tax (where employees physically work), and income tax nexus (where the business must file state income tax). High multi-state complexity industries: ecommerce (sales tax nexus across most states post-Wayfair), SaaS (state-specific SaaS taxability and nexus thresholds), construction (project sites across states create nexus), trucking (IFTA quarterly fuel tax across all US/Canadian jurisdictions), franchise operations (multi-unit operators across states), and any business with remote employees (payroll registration in every state where employees work).

      Multi-state work spans sales tax compliance, payroll management with multi-state setup, and coordination with the client’s CPA on income tax nexus opinions. Industry pages cover the specific multi-state patterns for each industry.

    5. Q.16 Which industries are most platform-dependent for accounting?

      Some industries run almost entirely from operational platforms that feed QuickBooks — the accounting follows the platform, not the other way around. Highly platform-dependent industries: home services (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Jobber, FieldEdge), fitness studios (MindBody, Mariana Tek, Glofox, ClubReady), restaurants (Toast, Square POS, Lightspeed, Clover), dental practices (Dentrix, Eaglesoft, Open Dental), healthcare practices (Epic, Athena, NextGen, Kareo), law firms (Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther), construction (Procore, Buildertrend, CoConstruct, Sage 100), ecommerce (Shopify, Amazon Seller Central, with A2X or Webgility connectors).

      For these industries, the engagement model is heavily about integration discipline: getting the platform-to-QuickBooks data flow correct, reconciling between platform reports and QuickBooks GL, handling the platform-specific revenue recognition. An industry-specialist firm knows the platforms by name and has built integration playbooks; a generic firm fumbles the platforms or treats them as afterthoughts.

    6. Q.17 Which industries need the most advisory layer on top of bookkeeping?

      Every industry benefits from advisory, but some industries have operational economics that make advisory especially valuable. High-advisory-value industries: SaaS (unit economics — CAC, LTV, gross retention — drive every strategic decision), agencies (utilization and AGI per FTE drive profitability), construction (project margin and WIP drive cash flow), restaurants (prime cost and labor management drive survival), home services (RPTD and recurring revenue drive valuation), professional services (utilization, realization, and AR drive cash), manufacturing (BOM costing, inventory turnover, gross margin discipline).

      For these industries, fractional CFO work often pays for itself within months by identifying specific operational improvements (pricing adjustment, capacity reallocation, working capital optimization) that produce $50K–$500K+ in annual margin. The discovery call assesses whether the business is ready to act on advisory analysis — advisory is wasted if leadership doesn’t use the work.

    1. Q.18 Which industries does TechBrot serve?

      TechBrot serves 17 industries across U.S. small and mid-market businesses, with dedicated industry pages and specialist depth in each: agencies (marketing, advertising, PR), construction, dental practices, ecommerce, fitness studios, franchise operations, healthcare practices, home services (HVAC, plumbing, electrical), law firms, manufacturing, nonprofits, professional services, real estate, restaurants, retail, SaaS, and trucking.

      Each industry has its own dedicated page with industry-specific challenges, sub-vertical breakdowns, integration platforms, KPI frameworks, FAQ content, and engagement scope. Industry experience matters because each industry has specific QuickBooks configurations, chart-of-accounts structures, and operational workflows that don’t translate cleanly across sectors.     2. Q.19 What groups the 17 industries we cover?

      The 17 industries cluster naturally into five groupings by accounting pattern. (1) Project-based operators: agencies, construction, law firms, professional services — share matter/project-level P&L, utilization metrics, WIP discipline. (2) Subscription / recurring revenue: SaaS, fitness, franchise, prepaid services in home services — share ASC 606 deferred revenue mechanics, MRR/ARR or analogous metrics, retention KPIs.

      (3) Inventory / product operators: ecommerce, retail, manufacturing, restaurant — share inventory accounting, COGS discipline, supplier reconciliation, multi-channel revenue. (4) Dispatched-service operators: home services, trucking — share crew/vehicle economics, dispatched-service KPIs, route-based revenue, equipment-heavy capital structure. (5) Regulated practitioners: dental, healthcare, nonprofits, real estate — share regulatory overlays, specialty platform integration, distinctive revenue cycles. Understanding the grouping helps identify which cross-industry patterns apply to your business.

    3. Q.20 What about industries adjacent to your 17 — insurance, consulting, hospitality?

      Several adjacent industries we cover under existing pages. Insurance agencies — covered under professional services for the standard agency model (commission-based revenue, carrier appointments, premium trust handling if applicable). Insurance brokerages with more complex revenue (MGA arrangements, profit-sharing, override commissions) we handle with custom scope. Consulting practices — covered under professional services with utilization and engagement-based reporting. Hospitality (hotels, B&Bs, short-term rentals) — partial overlap with restaurant (similar daily revenue tracking) and real estate (occupancy economics, property-level P&L); we handle smaller operators in this space.

      Veterinary practices — covered under healthcare patterns with practice management software (Cornerstone, AVImark) integration. Education and tutoring services — partial overlap with professional services and SaaS depending on model. Subscription boxes and DTC ecommerce — covered under ecommerce with subscription accounting overlay from SaaS patterns. The discovery call maps your business to the right combination.

    4. Q.21 Will TechBrot add coverage for my industry if it’s not on the list?

      The 17 industries currently covered reflect where we have built deep engagement experience and reusable playbooks. We expand coverage only when we have built genuine depth — we don’t claim an industry on the website just because we’d like the search traffic. Adding a published industry requires: multiple completed engagements in the industry, mapped CoA and KPI templates, identified key integration platforms, documented sub-verticals, and an active engagement model.

      That said, for businesses outside the current 17, we still handle engagements that fit close enough to existing patterns (see Q.20). If you’re in an industry we don’t cover and don’t fit any existing pattern well, the discovery call honestly assesses whether we’re the right firm or whether you’d be better served by a specialist. We’d rather decline the engagement than deliver mediocre work outside our depth. Industries we’re unlikely to add in the foreseeable future are listed in Q.22.

    5. Q.22 What industries do you not serve well?

      We’re explicit about coverage limits. Industries we don’t serve well:

      • Cannabis (separate legal scope, banking restrictions, state-by-state regulatory complexity we don’t specialize in)
      • Oil & gas operations (specialty depletion accounting, working-interest economics)
      • Insurance carrier accounting (heavily regulated statutory accounting, separate from agency-side which we do cover)
      • Financial services with regulatory overlay — broker-dealers, registered investment advisors, mortgage originators, money services businesses
      • Agriculture and farming operations (commodity hedging, crop insurance, USDA programs, farm-specific tax provisions)
      • International businesses with material foreign currency or transfer pricing complexity (cross-border tax structuring, multi-jurisdictional reporting)
      • Government contractors with DCAA compliance requirements (cost accounting standards, indirect rate audits)

      When industry fit isn’t a match, we say so on the discovery call and recommend specialists better positioned to serve. Honest scope is the moat — we’d rather decline an engagement than deliver mediocre work outside our depth.

    6. Q.23 Can you handle international or multi-country businesses?

      Partially — depends on the international complexity. US businesses with minor international exposure (some international customers paying in USD, a single foreign vendor, minor foreign-currency transactions): we handle this within standard engagements. US businesses with meaningful international operations (foreign subsidiaries, transfer pricing arrangements, multi-currency operations, foreign payroll, VAT/GST compliance in other countries): we handle the US side cleanly but coordinate with international specialists for the non-US side — this work falls outside our depth.

      Non-US businesses (incorporated outside the United States with US-only or US-primary operations): we can handle the US accounting layer for US tax compliance and US operations, but we don’t practice in any other country’s accounting standards or tax systems. Multi-country businesses with substantial cross-border complexity: not our depth — we recommend international accounting firms. The honest read: TechBrot is a US-focused independent ProAdvisor firm; we excel within that scope and acknowledge limits beyond it.

    Cluster 05

    Switching industries & new ventures.

    Business pivots, parallel ventures, holding-company structures, and what to do when industry classification changes over time.

    1. Q.24 What if my business is pivoting industries?

      Pivots happen — an agency builds productized services that become a SaaS business, a retailer shifts heavily to ecommerce, a restaurant launches a packaged-goods line. The accounting must follow. A pivot engagement typically includes: (1) revised CoA structure to handle both the legacy industry and the new direction, (2) revised revenue recognition methodology if the new model has different ASC 606 patterns, (3) new platform integrations for the new business mode (a SaaS platform on top of agency operations, a Shopify integration on top of retail), (4) revised KPI dashboard reflecting the metrics that now matter, (5) revised monthly review focus.

      The transition is often best handled as a one-time restructure engagement followed by ongoing monthly bookkeeping in the new structure. We’ve handled enough pivots to do this cleanly without forcing the business to rebuild books from scratch.

    2. Q.25 What if I’m launching a parallel business in a different industry?

      Common with successful operators — the original business is established and the founder launches a second business in an adjacent or unrelated industry. Two structural approaches: (1) Same entity, separated tracking — both businesses in one legal entity with Class or Department tracking to separate them in QuickBooks. Simpler legally and operationally but doesn’t fully separate liability. (2) Separate legal entities — new LLC or corporation for the new business, with intercompany transactions tracked between entities. More overhead but cleaner liability separation, simpler exit later.

      The right structure depends on liability concerns, expected scale, tax planning (coordinate with your CPA on this), and whether the businesses will likely separate later. For most parallel ventures we recommend separate entities once the new business is generating meaningful revenue ($100K+ annual). The engagement scope grows to handle both businesses — pricing reflects the added complexity but typically scales sub-linearly because we already understand the operator and the underlying entity structure.

    3. Q.26 How do you handle holding-company structures across industries?

      Holding-company structures are common for operators with multiple businesses, often across different industries. Standard engagement structure: holding company (typically an LLC or LP) for ownership and consolidation; operating subsidiaries as separate legal entities, each in its own industry. Each subsidiary maintains its own QuickBooks file with industry-specific structure; the holding company maintains its own minimal QuickBooks file primarily for intercompany transactions and equity flows.

      Consolidated reporting rolls up across all entities for a unified holding-level view; subsidiary-level reporting maintains industry-specific structure for operational use. We handle holding-company engagements as multi-entity scope with industry-specialist treatment per subsidiary. Common patterns we see: real estate holding companies with multiple property LLCs plus an operating company; family offices with mixed operating businesses; serial entrepreneurs with multiple ventures; private equity individual operators rolling up acquisitions. See fractional CFO for coordinated holding-level financial leadership.

    4. Q.27 My CPA categorizes my business as one industry but it operates as another — what do I do?

      This happens often. Tax classification (NAICS code, principal business activity on the tax return) sometimes lags how a business actually operates — particularly after pivots or new ventures. The tax-side classification matters for tax filings; the operational classification matters for accounting depth and KPI design.

      The fix is dual: (1) Operationally, we design CoA, integrations, and KPIs around how the business actually runs — not the tax classification. (2) For tax filing, your CPA may want to update the NAICS code on the return to reflect current operations, which can have implications for industry-specific tax provisions, audit risk, and IRS classification. The two layers don’t need to be identical, but they should be coherent — we coordinate with your CPA so operational reporting and tax classification tell the same story without contradicting each other.

    Cluster 06

    Technology by industry.

    Which QuickBooks plan fits which industry, the industry-specific integration platforms we work with, and how the technology stack actually comes together.

    1. Q.28 Which QuickBooks plan fits which industries?

      Rough mapping by typical business stage. QuickBooks Online (QBO): most service businesses regardless of industry (agencies, professional services, law firms, healthcare practices, smaller restaurants and home services operations), early-stage SaaS startups, and most small ecommerce businesses. QuickBooks Desktop: increasingly rare for new businesses but still appropriate for established operators with industry-specific Desktop workflows, particularly in manufacturing and construction running legacy ERP-style operations.

      QuickBooks Enterprise: larger operations with advanced inventory (multi-location retail, growing manufacturing, mid-market ecommerce), 25+ employee counts, multi-entity structures, or industry-specific Enterprise editions (Contractor for construction, Manufacturing for manufacturers, Retail for retailers, Nonprofit for nonprofits). The discovery call assesses fit based on your specific operations, not generic revenue thresholds. See /quickbooks/which-plan-is-right/ for the full decision framework.

    3. Q.30 What if my industry needs a platform you don’t work with?

      We work with most major platforms in our covered industries and can typically learn an unfamiliar platform if it’s in an industry we already serve. The threshold question is whether the platform has a viable QuickBooks integration path — some platforms export clean data we can import, some have native QuickBooks sync, some require manual journal entry from monthly reports. We work with any of these.

      Platforms we genuinely struggle with: truly proprietary or custom-built ERPs with no QuickBooks integration and no clean export, industry-specific platforms in industries we don’t cover (where we’d need to learn both the platform and the industry from scratch), and platforms with security restrictions that prevent ProAdvisor access (rare but exists). The discovery call assesses platform fit honestly — if your stack genuinely doesn’t work with our model, we’ll say so. Otherwise, integration setup is part of standard engagement scope, not a separate fee.

    1. Q.31 What does an industry-aware discovery call cover?

      (4) Honest assessment — whether TechBrot is actually the right firm. The discovery is genuinely complimentary — no pressure to commit on the call, no high-pressure sales tactics.
