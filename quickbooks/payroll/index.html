    QuickBooks Payroll, in five questions.

    What are the QuickBooks Payroll plans?

    QuickBooks Online Payroll has three tiers: Core (full-service payroll, automated federal/state tax filing, direct deposit), Premium (adds same-day direct deposit, HR support center, workers’ comp admin, time tracking), Elite (adds tax-penalty protection, personal HR advisor, project time tracking). Desktop Payroll exists for Desktop users. Right tier depends on employee count + multi-state complexity, not the priciest default.

    Can TechBrot set up QuickBooks Payroll?

    Yes. Certified Intuit Payroll ProAdvisors configure it end-to-end: company/employee setup, federal + state registration mapping, local jurisdiction tax (CAGIT/COIT, RITA, PA local PSD), pay schedules, deductions, direct deposit, prior-payroll entry for mid-year. Fixed-fee, written scope.

    Why is QuickBooks Payroll calculating wrong tax?

    Most common: wrong work/residence state, multi-state employees without reciprocity/allocation, missing local taxes, stale withholding tables, or wrong filing status. Multi-state and local-tax errors are most frequent — and most expensive, since they surface as state notices. A ProAdvisor audits config, identifies exposure, prepares corrections.

    How is multi-state payroll handled?

    Configure each employee’s work state + residence state, apply reciprocity, allocate wages across states, register withholding in each required state, configure local taxes — verified against actual state rules, not QuickBooks defaults. This is where generic setup most often fails.

    Can I switch to QuickBooks Payroll from another provider?

    Yes. Migrating from Gusto, ADP, or Paychex transfers employee records, YTD totals, tax registration, and pay history so filings stay accurate. Cleanest at quarter/year start; mid-year routine with proper YTD entry. Fixed-fee project; first live run verified before completion.

    Where it goes wrong

    Multi-state & local tax: the part QuickBooks Payroll won't fix for you.

    QuickBooks Payroll automates filing — but only based on how it’s configured. The software files exactly what you tell it to, which means a configuration error becomes a filing error becomes a state notice. The failures cluster in predictable places:

    Work state vs. residence state. An employee living in one state and working in another needs both configured, with the correct withholding for each. QuickBooks doesn’t infer this — if it’s set wrong, withholding is wrong from day one.

    Reciprocity agreements. Many neighboring states have reciprocity (the employee pays tax only to their residence state). If reciprocity exists and isn’t applied, the employee is double-withheld; if it doesn’t exist and is wrongly assumed, the employer under-withholds.

    Local jurisdiction tax. This is the deepest trap. Indiana has county income tax (CAGIT/COIT) tied to the employee’s January 1 county of residence. Ohio has municipal tax administered through RITA and CCA. Pennsylvania has local earned-income tax keyed to PSD codes. New York City and Yonkers have resident taxes. QuickBooks Payroll supports these — but only if a human configures them correctly. They’re the single most common thing left unconfigured.

    A Certified Payroll ProAdvisor sets these against the actual state and local requirements and verifies them — the difference between payroll that’s quietly correct and payroll that surfaces as a notice 12 months later. See ongoing payroll management →

    Common QuickBooks Payroll issues

    What we’re called in to fix.

    Recurring QuickBooks Payroll problems — nearly all configuration, not software.

    • Wrong tax withheld.

      Federal or state withholding off, usually traceable to a wrong filing status, exemption, or work/residence state on the employee record.

    • Missing local taxes.

      County or municipal taxes (CAGIT/COIT, RITA, PA local) never configured — discovered when a local notice arrives.

    • Multi-state employee chaos.

      Employees across state lines without reciprocity or wage allocation set, producing wrong withholding in both states.

    • Mid-year migration gaps.

      Switched providers mid-year without correct YTD entry, so quarterly and W-2 totals don’t reconcile.

    • W-2 / 1099 corrections.

      Year-end forms wrong because of accumulated configuration errors — requiring W-2c or corrected 1099 filings.

    • Contractor misclassification.

      Workers paid as 1099 who should be W-2 (or vice versa), set up wrong in QuickBooks and creating exposure.
