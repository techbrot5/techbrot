## VERIFIED MICHIGAN TAX FACTS (with sources)
- **State individual income tax — a FLAT 4.25% rate.** Michigan levies a single **flat 4.25%** individual income tax on
  individuals and fiduciaries. The State Treasurer, Senate Fiscal Agency, and House Fiscal Agency **confirmed the rate
  stays 4.25% for the 2026 tax year** (the FY2025 general-fund growth did not exceed inflation, so no rate-reduction
  trigger applied). This is the clean MI contrast to OH (mid-phase-out toward zero) and to the graduated NJ/MD/VA
  ladders: Michigan's rate is flat and stable. A personal exemption applies (per-exemption amount set annually — keep
  QUALITATIVE, link the Treasury rate page). Source: Michigan Dept of Treasury, "4.25% Income Tax Rate for Individuals
  and Fiduciaries in 2026 Tax Year" (taxpayer notice, Apr 15 2026); MCL 206.51 (Income Tax Act).
- **City income tax — THE genuinely MI-distinct local tax; the hook.** **24 Michigan cities impose a city income tax**
  under the **City Income Tax Act (CITA, 1964 PA 284 / MCL 141.501 et seq.)**. Mechanics (VERIFIED):
  - **Rate tiers.** **Detroit** is the top tier: **2.4% resident / 1.2% nonresident**. **Grand Rapids** and **Saginaw**:
    **1.5% resident / 0.75% nonresident**. **Highland Park**: **2% / 1%**. The other **~20 cities** are the CITA base
    tier: **1% resident / 0.5% nonresident** (keep individual non-Detroit rates QUALITATIVE; link the Treasury list /
    the city). The statutory CITA caps a city at 1% resident / 0.5% nonresident unless separately authorized (Detroit,
    Grand Rapids, Saginaw, Highland Park are the authorized exceptions).
  - **The 24 cities** (name them; rates beyond Detroit framed qualitatively): **Albion, Battle Creek, Benton Harbor, Big
    Rapids, Detroit, East Lansing, Flint, Grand Rapids, Grayling, Hamtramck, Highland Park, Hudson, Ionia, Jackson,
    Lansing, Lapeer, Muskegon, Muskegon Heights, Pontiac, Port Huron, Portland, Saginaw, Springfield, Walker.**
  - **Work-location withholding for nonresidents.** A nonresident is generally subject to a city's tax (and the employer
    to withholding) for work physically performed **in that city** — for Detroit, only if Detroit is the employee's
    **predominant place of employment** (25%+ of compensation). Residents are taxed on all income by their home city.
    This is the MI analog to Ohio's municipal tax, but **far simpler** (24 cities, not 649) — still a real per-employee
    work-location withholding setup that generic payroll gets wrong.
  - **Administration.** **Detroit's** city income tax is **administered by the Michigan Dept of Treasury**; **Flint**
    moves to Treasury administration **effective Jan 1, 2027**; the remaining cities self-administer under CITA. Confirm
    a city's collector before registering and withholding.
  - **HONEST FRAMING:** TechBrot configures QuickBooks Payroll for city-tax work-location withholding per employee; the
    city / Treasury filing is made by the business or its CPA. Source: Michigan Dept of Treasury — City Tax
    (`michigan.gov/taxes/citytax`); "Which cities impose an income tax?"; City of Detroit Individual Income Tax; City
    Income Tax Act (MCL 141.501 et seq.).
- **Corporate Income Tax (CIT) — a real 6% corporate income tax.** Michigan levies a **6% Corporate Income Tax** on **C
  corporations** (and entities taxed as corporations federally) with nexus in Michigan, on the apportioned CIT base.
  This is a genuine corporate income tax — the clean contrast to **Ohio (no corporate income tax; the CAT
  gross-receipts tax instead)**. (Michigan repealed the gross-receipts-style Michigan Business Tax; the CIT replaced
  it.) Flow-through entities are not subject to the CIT (they flow to the owners / the FTE election below). Source:
  Michigan Dept of Treasury — Corporate Income Tax (`michigan.gov/taxes/business-taxes/cit`); MCL 206.601 et seq.
- **Flow-Through Entity (FTE) Tax — the advisory angle (SALT-cap workaround).** Michigan's **elective Flow-Through Entity
  tax** lets a qualifying partnership or S-corp pay tax **at the entity level at the same rate as the individual income
  tax (4.25%)**, so owners can deduct the state tax federally and claim a refundable MI credit. The **election is made
  by an electronic payment through Michigan Treasury Online (MTO)** and is **effective for three years** (the year of
  election plus the next two). Advisory/coordination item — the CPA makes the election and files Form 5772; TechBrot
  keeps the books FTE-ready. Source: Michigan Dept of Treasury — Flow-Through Entity Tax (FAQ); Income Tax Act § 51 /
  Form 5772.
- **Sales & use tax — a FLAT 6%, NO local sales tax.** Michigan's sales and use tax is **6% statewide**, and **Michigan
  does NOT allow any city, county, or local unit to impose a sales or use tax** (the rate is constitutionally
  constrained). So unlike Ohio's county-variable combined rate, **every Michigan sale is 6% — one rate, statewide**.
  The matching **use tax is 6%** (out-of-state purchases / items brought into the state; remote-seller and marketplace
  nexus apply). The clean MI message: simple rate, but get nexus and taxability right. Source: Michigan Dept of Treasury
  — Sales and Use Taxes (`michigan.gov/taxes/business-taxes/sales-use-tax`); MCL 205.51 et seq. (GSTA) / MCL 205.91 et
  seq. (Use Tax Act).
- **Reciprocity — IL/IN/KY/MN/OH/WI (income tax only; CITY tax NOT covered).** Michigan has income-tax reciprocal
  agreements with **Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Minnesota, Ohio, and Wisconsin**: a resident of those states working in
  Michigan is **exempt from Michigan income tax and Michigan withholding** on wages (and a Michigan resident is exempt
  in the reciprocal state), so the employee files **Form MI-W4** claiming the exemption and the employer withholds for
  the home state. **CRITICAL:** reciprocity covers the **state** income tax on **compensation only** — it does **NOT**
  apply to **city income taxes** (a reciprocal-state resident working in Detroit can still owe the Detroit city tax),
  does **not** apply to **independent contractors**, and does **not** cover non-wage income. Source: Michigan Dept of
  Treasury — Reciprocal Agreements (RAB 2017-13); 446 Michigan Income Tax Withholding Guide.
