## VERIFIED COLORADO TAX FACTS (with sources)
- **Flat 4.40% income tax (individual AND corporate) — for 2025.** Colorado taxes all Colorado taxable income at a **single
  flat 4.40% rate** for tax year 2025; the **corporate** income tax is also a flat **4.40%**. The rate is subject to a
  **temporary TABOR-surplus reduction mechanism** (active 2025&ndash;2035): in a year with a large TABOR surplus the rate can
  drop &mdash; it was **temporarily reduced to 4.25% for tax year 2024**, then returned to **4.40% for 2025**. Frame: **"a flat
  4.40% (for 2025; some years see a temporary TABOR-surplus reduction &mdash; confirm the current year with the Colorado Dept
  of Revenue)."** Unlike Washington/Tennessee, Colorado HAS an income tax, so **payroll DOES carry Colorado state income-tax
  withholding** (Form DR 1098 / DR 0004). Source: Colorado Dept of Revenue &mdash; Individual Income Tax; CO General Assembly
  (HB24-1065 / TABOR refund mechanism).
- **The home-rule self-collecting city sales tax — THE CO-distinct hook (the most fragmented sales-tax system in the US).**
  Colorado&rsquo;s **state sales-tax rate is just 2.9%** (one of the lowest state rates in the country), but **counties, cities,
  and special districts (RTD, scientific/cultural, etc.) stack on top**, so the **combined rate commonly lands around
  7%&ndash;9%+** by location. The complexity: about **70+ "home-rule" cities are self-collecting** &mdash; Denver, Colorado
  Springs, Aurora, Boulder, Fort Collins, Lakewood, and more **administer their OWN sales and use tax**, with their **own
  rules, returns, and registration** separate from the state. Many home-rule cities now participate in the state&rsquo;s
  **SUTS (Sales &amp; Use Tax System)** &mdash; a single portal to file one consolidated return for the state and participating
  home-rule cities &mdash; but **not all do**, so some cities still require a separate return. Colorado is **destination-sourced**
  (since Oct 1, 2022): tax to where the buyer takes possession; a small-business exception applied under $100,000 in retail
  sales. Keep specific combined/city rates **qualitative**; link the source. Source: Colorado Dept of Revenue &mdash; Local
  Government Sales Tax; SUTS; Sales Tax Guide; Destination Sourcing.
- **The Retail Delivery Fee (RDF) — a CO-distinct compliance quirk.** Colorado charges a **per-retail-delivery fee** on each
  retail sale delivered by motor vehicle that includes at least one item of taxable tangible personal property. The fee is
  **$0.28 per delivery for July 2025&ndash;June 2026**, and it is **adjusted for inflation each July** (the Dept publishes the
  new rate by April 15). **Qualified small and new businesses are exempt** (the fee no longer applies to qualifying small/new
  businesses under the sales threshold). Retailers can either add it as a line item or pay it themselves. Keep the exact
  current-year amount tied to the period and link the source. Source: Colorado Dept of Revenue &mdash; Retail Delivery Fee; RDF
  Rates; SB23-143.
- **SALT Parity Act (PTE election) — the advisory angle.** Under the **SALT Parity Act (SB22-124)**, a partnership or S-corp
  can **elect to pay Colorado income tax at the entity level** so the owners can deduct it federally (a SALT-cap workaround).
  Advisory/coordination item &mdash; the CPA makes the election (Form DR 1705 / DR 0106) and files; TechBrot keeps the books
  PTE-ready. Source: Colorado Dept of Revenue &mdash; SALT Parity Act Election; SB22-124.
- **No local income tax (the local complexity is sales tax, not income).** Colorado has **no city or county income tax**, so
  the local fragmentation is entirely on the **sales-tax** side (home-rule cities), not payroll income tax. Colorado state
  income-tax withholding still applies on wages. (A handful of cities historically levied a small flat "occupational privilege
  tax" / head tax, e.g. Denver&rsquo;s OPT &mdash; mention only qualitatively if relevant; it is a small per-employee monthly
  tax, not an income tax.) Source: Colorado Dept of Revenue &mdash; Withholding.
